To Wake the People

Why NAD?

April 1, 2015 by Uzzie T. Cannon

Novels of the African Diaspora is an online catalog of novels written by people of African descent throughout the world. Its purpose is to collectively represent these novels and to offer digital tools general readers and scholars can use to understand the development of the genre. NAD will allow users to not only pose problems surrounding the relevant subject matter of the various novels, but it will also help answer the following questions and more: how do the authors of these novels fit into a greater tradition; can we reimagine this tradition; when and where is this literature produced and under what circumstances; beyond the subjective focus of the project, what other commonalities can be gleaned from the meta-data that is collected on these novels? Novels of the African Diaspora online project will help increase the reading of the cataloged novels and hopefully change the way scholars critically engage with the texts.

So why then did I decide to create this project and where is it going? One of my concerns after entering the digital humanities as a scholar was the lack of projects involving 20th and 21th Century African American literature. Since this is my primary teaching and research area, I felt compelled to focus on ways of digitally interacting with the literature that I like to teach and read. I found that many DH projects revolve around Early Modern and Modern texts of mainstream British and American literatures. And I understand why--both areas have texts dating back to the invention of the printing press that need to be remediated for longevity and greater scholarly access. Also on the side of these literary periods is the loss of copyrights; they were all published before 1922 in this country and thus are in the public domain.

Consequently, I had to find a way to enter the DH and make an impact with the tools that exist and the focus that I bring. Challenged by copyright laws more than anything else, I am not able to upload fulltexts for mining; I must use general bibliographic information and the sociology of literary texts to enter the domanins of serious DH research. I'm not deterred, however; I think there are still many opportunities to work with my research periods in the digital humanities. I just have to create my owns ways in: catalogs, data visualizations, and maps. These tools are quite powerful when used properly, so I am trying to learn as much as possible about client-side and simple server-side programming to make them useful in my projects.

By the way, NAD is purposefully web-based because I want all those interested in novels written by those of the African Diaspora to have open source access to the tools. As the site grows, it will offer the opportunity for forum discussion so that we can create moments for collaboration on other projects relevant to literary novels.

About

Uzzie Cannon is the project designer and developer for NAD. She has a Ph.D. in English with a specialization in African American literature and culture. She is also a Digital Humanist with an interest in client-side design for web applications for literature and composition and digital humanities pedagogy.
